THE CENTRAL THESIS: MORE MONEY · SAME CRISIS · SYSTEMIC FAILURE

Between FY 2000–01 and FY 2025–26, the Union Government's agricultural budget has grown from approximately ₹18,600 crore to ₹1,27,290 crore — a nominal increase of over 580%. Three governments, across three ideological dispensations, have promised to transform farming, eliminate debt, and secure farmer incomes. The Vajpayee government built rural infrastructure. The Singh government launched MGNREGA, the largest rural employment programme in world history. The Modi government introduced PM-KISAN, the world's largest direct income transfer scheme for farmers, and tripled agricultural credit targets.

And yet: 3,94,206 farmers and agricultural labourers have died by suicide between 1995 and 2023 (NCRB). More than half of all farm households remain indebted. The average farm household earns ₹10,218 per month — less than the minimum wage floor in 14 Indian states. This is not the failure of any single party. It is the failure of an approach to agriculture that treats subsidies as substitutes for structural reform.

This report traces, with 26 years of verified data, how this contradiction was constructed — and who pays the price.

02 The Suicide Timeline: 28 Years of Unbroken Crisis ⚠ Crisis

No war, no epidemic, no natural disaster has claimed more lives in Indian agriculture over this period. Three governments, seven Prime Ministerial terms, dozens of welfare schemes — and the count continues.

03 GDP Contribution: The Long Structural Decline Macro

Agriculture's share in India's GDP has declined from ~22% in FY 2000 to ~17.6% in FY 2025 — while the absolute sector grew significantly. However, this decline reflects structural transition, not sectoral prosperity: services grew faster, not because agriculture improved farmers' lives, but because manufacturing and services expanded from a low base. The average farm worker's productivity remains ~3× lower than the national worker average.

05 Green Flags & Red Flags: What Actually Worked vs. What Failed ⚑ Verdict
🟢 Green Flags — What Showed Evidence of Impact
MGNREGA (2005): Empirically linked to ~40% reduction in farmer suicides between 2010–2019. Kerala: 1,118 → 105. West Bengal: zero by 2012. World's largest rural employment guarantee. Reduced emergency debt from moneylenders.
Kisan Credit Card (KCC, 1998): Reduced moneylender dependence for short-term credit. 7.75 Cr operational accounts (Mar 2024). Institutional share of agri credit grew from ~40% (1990) to ~69.6% (2024).
Foodgrain Production Growth: From 209 MT (FY00) to 357.73 MT (FY25) — a 71% increase over 25 years. Rice and wheat at all-time records. Self-sufficiency in most staples achieved.
Agricultural Export Growth: From ~$8B (FY00) to $51.9B (FY25) — a 6× increase. India now 9th globally in agri exports. Rice export at $12.47B — record high.
PM-KISAN Income Support: ₹3.46 lakh crore transferred to 11 crore farmers in 6 years. Direct benefit transfer (DBT) with minimal leakage. Added income floor for small/marginal farmers.
Irrigated Area Expansion: From ~55 Mha (FY00) to 75.3 Mha (FY25) — +37% over 25 years. PMKSY and micro-irrigation schemes reduced weather vulnerability in targeted regions.
🔴 Red Flags — What Failed Despite Massive Spending
MSP System (1965–2026): MSP announced for 23 crops. Effective procurement: only wheat and rice. For pulses, oilseeds, cotton — farmers receive 20–40% below MSP in distress years. No legal guarantee even after 26 years and three governments.
Debt Waiver (2008, ₹71,000 Cr): Largest ever. But one-time only. Did not prevent re-indebtedness. By 2018–19, average outstanding loan back to ₹74,121/household. Waivers treated symptoms; ignored root causes.
Crop Insurance (PMFBY, 2016): Only ~30% of non-loanee farmers enrolled. Claim settlement delays of 1–2 years documented. Premium subsidy capture by insurance companies without commensurate payout. States have started opting out (AP, West Bengal, Gujarat).
PM-KISAN Income Gap: ₹6,000/year = ₹500/month per household. Input costs (fertilisers, seeds, diesel) have risen 60–80% since 2014. Net real income effect negligible against rising cost of cultivation. The scheme is income support, not income transformation.
Cold Chain / Post-Harvest: 40% of horticultural produce lost post-harvest (APEDA). Despite ₹800+ crore annual schemes, cold chain covers <5% of perishable output. Value destruction: ₹90,000–1,00,000 crore annually. No government has systematically addressed this.
Swaminathan Commission (2006): Recommended C2+50% MSP, land reform, irrigation, cooperative farming. Submitted to UPA-II. Shelved. Cited by farmer unions during NDA-II farm law protests. 18+ years — zero implementation. Arguably the clearest policy failure of independent India's agricultural governance.

07 Systemic Fault Lines — Why Money Alone Cannot Fix This ⚑ Diagnosis
The Structural Scissors: Budget ↑ · Farmer Income (Real) ↗ (slowly) · Debt ↑ · Suicides (Persistent)
Normalised indices (FY2000 = 100): All four variables tracked over 26 years — the divergence between spending and welfare outcomes
Index: Budget Allocation (MoF), Farmer Suicides (NCRB), Real Farm Income (NSSO/NABARD — interpolated), Agricultural Household Debt (NSSO). Normalised to 100 at FY2000 base.
F1
Land Fragmentation Crisis (Unaddressed by All Governments): Average landholding has shrunk from 2.3 ha (1960) to ~1.07 ha (2024). At this size, mechanisation is economically unviable, input costs per unit of output are higher, and risk absorption capacity is near zero. 86% of India's 140 million farm households are small/marginal. No government in 26 years has introduced meaningful land consolidation policy, cooperative cultivation incentives, or economies-of-scale models at scale. This is the foundational structural problem that every budget line ignores.
F2
MSP: The 57-Year-Old Promise Never Kept (Across All Governments): MSP has been announced for 23 crops since 1966. Effective government procurement exists only for wheat and rice. For 20+ other crops — pulses, oilseeds, millets, cotton — farmers must sell in mandis at market rates that frequently fall below MSP. Swaminathan Commission (2006) recommended legal guarantee of C2+50% MSP. Neither UPA-II nor NDA-II/III has implemented this. Farm Laws (2020), which could have expanded market access, were repealed after protests — without replacing them with the statutory MSP guarantee that farmers demanded.
F3
Post-Harvest Loss Economy (₹1 Lakh Crore Annual Destruction): India loses ~40% of horticultural produce and 5–7% of foodgrains annually to post-harvest losses (APEDA/ICAR). This represents ~₹90,000–1,00,000 crore of value destruction per year — approximately equal to 75% of the entire agriculture ministry budget. Cold chain infrastructure covers less than 5% of perishable output. Despite multiple schemes (MIDH, PMKSY, AIF), the cold chain gap has not been systematically addressed in any government's term. The produce of the farmer is destroyed before it reaches the market.
F4
Input Cost Inflation (Unchecked for 26 Years): Fertiliser prices rose 300–400% between 2000 and 2024 (DAP price: ₹4,830/bag in FY00 to ₹27,000+/bag in FY23 before partial subsidy rollback). Seed costs up 150–200%. Diesel 180% (direct farm machinery cost). MSP revisions have not kept pace with the C2 cost of cultivation for most crops. Every government has subsidised inputs — but subsidies consistently lag cost increases, and subsidy efficiency is poor (broad-acre fertiliser subsidy benefits large farmers disproportionately).
F5
Regional Credit Inequality (A Structural NABARD Failure): Southern India (17% of cropped area) receives 48% of total agricultural credit. Eastern and Central India — Bihar, Jharkhand, Chhattisgarh, eastern UP — where small/marginal farmer concentration is highest, receive a disproportionately small share. The KCC scheme, while nationally scaled, is effectively concentrated in states with stronger banking infrastructure. The farmers most in need of formal credit remain most dependent on informal moneylenders charging 24–36% annual interest.
F6
NCRB Data Suppression (2016–2019): The National Crime Records Bureau did not publish its "Accidental Deaths & Suicides in India" report for the years 2016, 2017, and 2018 for nearly three years, releasing consolidated data only in 2019–2020. This is not an administrative delay — this is the most widely cited source of farmer suicide data, and its suppression during a period of intensifying agrarian distress (farm loan waivers, Vidarbha crisis sequel, input cost spikes) constitutes a governance accountability failure. Transparency in crisis data is itself a policy obligation.
07B Rural Welfare Indicators: Income, Consumption, Debt & Health ⚠ Welfare

The macro output story — record crops, rising exports, bigger budgets — dissolves when measured against rural household-level welfare indicators. Four variables expose the true condition of India's farming families: (1) Monthly Per Capita Consumption Expenditure, which proxies real living standards; (2) average rural household debt outstanding; (3) agricultural labour productivity (GVA per agri worker); and (4) rural health spending — perhaps the most understated signal of rural distress. Together, these form the missing half of India's agricultural data narrative.

The Productivity Chasm — Wider in 2025 Than in 2000: In FY 2000, an agricultural worker generated approximately 2.4× less economic value than the average national worker. In FY 2025, that gap is ~3.0×. Despite 580% budget growth, the relative productivity disadvantage of farming has not narrowed — it has widened. This means farmers are not just poor in absolute terms; they are increasingly poor relative to the rest of the economy. No government policy framework across 26 years has directly targeted agricultural labour productivity through mechanisation subsidies, cooperative farming at scale, or consolidation incentives for small landholders.
🔴 Rural Health: The Hidden Debt Driver
OOP health spending (FY00): 62–65% of total health expenditure borne out-of-pocket by households. Rural = worse. Single hospitalisation can cost ₹15,000–50,000 — equivalent to 1.5–5× monthly farm income.
NSSO SAS 2018–19 finding: 22% of Kisan Credit Card (KCC) loan holders reported using agricultural credit for medical expenses. This is borrowed money meant for seeds/inputs being spent on healthcare — a textbook indicator of a missing social protection floor.
NABARD NAFIS 2021–22: Of rural households with outstanding debt, medical emergencies ranked among the top 3 reasons for borrowing — alongside farming inputs and marriages.
India's public health spend: Only ~2.1% of GDP (2021–22, NHA) — among the lowest in G20. WHO recommends minimum 5%. Rural primary health centres face 30–40% doctor vacancy rates. The burden falls on household savings — which farmers do not have.
🟢 Rural MPCE: What Did Improve (Conditionally)
Nominal 8× growth: Rural MPCE grew from ₹486 (1999–00) to ₹3,773 (2022–23) — a near 8-fold nominal increase over 23 years.
Food security partially addressed: Share of food in MPCE fell from ~60% (2000) to 46.4% (2022–23), partly because NFSA provides free/subsidised grain to 80 crore people — reducing cash food expenditure burden.
NABARD NAFIS 2021–22: Average rural household monthly income rose from ₹8,059 (2016–17) to ₹12,698 (2021–22) — a 57.5% nominal growth, slightly above nominal GDP growth of 9% CAGR in the same period.
Poverty headcount declined: Rural poverty fell from ~37% (2004–05) to ~15–18% (2022–23, various estimates). MGNREGA, PM-KISAN, and food security programmes contributed to this floor-level improvement.
The Composite Welfare Picture (Across All 26 Years): Rural Indians are consuming more, earning more nominally, and are less likely to be in extreme poverty than in 2000. However: their debt has grown 16× (₹7,539 → ₹1,25,000 per household), their productivity gap versus urban workers has widened, their health expenditures remain catastrophic when illness strikes, and the consumption gap between rural and urban India has grown from 43% to 71%. The floor has risen; the ceiling has not. Agricultural policy has successfully prevented famine but has failed to engineer prosperity.

09 Conclusions: The System Is Not Broken — It Is Working As Designed ⚑ Verdict
The Central Finding of This Report:

India's agricultural policy system over 26 years has been extraordinarily effective at one thing: producing macro-level metrics that satisfy political communication needs — record production, rising budgets, growing exports — while preserving the structural conditions that ensure farming remains economically unviable for 86% of farmers. This is not malice. It is institutional capture by the most tractable variables: output, credit disbursement, budget size. The hardest variables — land fragmentation, price realisation, post-harvest infrastructure, effective MSP, rural wage growth — remain unaddressed across all three governments because they require structural reform that disrupts existing power configurations (traders, intermediaries, fertiliser industry, large-farm lobbies).

Every rupee spent on schemes without fixing these structural variables is a rupee that diminishes in impact.
C1
Budget ≠ Impact: The 580% increase in agricultural budget from FY00 to FY26 has not produced a proportional improvement in farmer welfare. Real farm household income grew ~40–50% over 25 years; urban informal income grew ~120%. The rural-urban income gap is wider today than in FY2000.
C2
MGNREGA is the Most Effective Agricultural Policy in 26 Years: Not a farming scheme, not a credit scheme, not an insurance scheme — a wage employment scheme — is the most empirically validated intervention in reducing agricultural distress. This tells us something profound about what farmers actually need: income certainty, not subsidised inputs.
C3
Record Production and Persistent Suicide Coexist Because They Measure Different Things: Foodgrain output measures aggregate national production. Farmer welfare measures individual household income, debt, and livelihood security. The former has been the policy objective. The latter is the human reality. Until policy is evaluated against welfare outcomes rather than production metrics, this divergence will persist.
C4
Data Transparency Itself Is a Policy Variable: The suppression of NCRB farmer suicide data for 2016–2019, the absence of a universal crop-loss compensation database, and the non-publication of effective (not announced) MSP realisation data across crops — these are not administrative oversights. They are governance choices that protect policy from accountability.
